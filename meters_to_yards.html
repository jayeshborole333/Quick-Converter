Meter

Definition: A meter (symbol: m) is the SI base unit for length and distance. It is currently defined as the distance that light travels in a vacuum in 1/299,792,458 seconds. This modern definition ensures a high degree of precision in scientific and engineering measurements.

History/origin: The meter was introduced in 1793 in France as one ten-millionth of the distance from the equator to the North Pole along a meridian through Paris. Over the years, it was redefined several times: initially via a platinum-iridium meter bar, then in 1960 using krypton-86 radiation wavelength, and finally in 1983 based on the speed of light, making it more stable and accurate.

Current use: The meter is globally recognized and used in almost all countries for daily activities and scientific measurements. It serves as the foundation for all other metric length units (centimeters, kilometers, etc.) and is used in areas such as engineering, architecture, construction, and more.

Yard

Definition: A yard (symbol: yd) is a unit of length in both the US customary and imperial systems, defined as exactly 0.9144 meters since 1959. It is also equivalent to 3 feet or 36 inches.

History/origin: The term “yard” dates back to Old English and may be derived from the word “gyrd” or “gerd,” meaning a stick or rod used for measuring. Historically, it was based on the length of a man's belt or girdle. Standardization occurred in the 12th century by King Henry I, who proclaimed the yard should be the distance from his nose to his outstretched thumb.

Current use: The yard is widely used in the United States and United Kingdom, particularly in sports (e.g., American football, golf, and soccer), construction, and road signs. While most of the world has shifted to the metric system, yards still remain in use for specific applications.
